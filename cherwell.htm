Orient expression

Sheila Cordner

Review from Cherwell

Audiences disappointed to view the Oxford's performances of Medea might be slightly relieved upon hearing there's another play laden with oestrogen-filled shrieks in town. Tensions and tragedy reminiscent of Greek drama permeate throughout Tsao Yu's Thunderstorm-a prominent modern Chinese play depicting the conflict latent in society moving away from a class-conscious patriarchy.

Chou Pu-Yuan, the capitalist master of an incest-filled household in north China, proves ultimately defenceless against his colourfully scandalous past. "The head of the house, a self-made man whose preoccupation with success has made him insensible to the ties of blood and affection, still bears within him the stifling weight of the old feudal society," director Albert Wu explains. As his work continues to consume him, family members attempt to overthrow the oppressive order Chou Pu-Yuan asserts. For starters, the wife of this high-ranking control freak, Fan Yi, falls for her stepson, Chou Ping. Does Chou Ping profess his love for her in return? Of course not; he falls for a servant girl, who, as part of a twisted family tree, turns out to be his sister.

In desperation , Fan Yi seeks to maintain the relationship with her tension as a sole way of coping with the environment in which she remains entrapped. Upon discovering Chou Ping's plan to elope with servant girl, Fan Yi becomes hysterical, initiation a downward spiral of madness intensifying until the play's conclusion. She reveals the elopement to her husband, who in turn unveils the true identity of his son's servant lover. Fan Yi, portrayed by Carly Vandenberg, undoubtedly functions as the eye of this storm. Vandenberg captures both the strong-willed and pathetic aspects of a woman bound to a much older, domineering husband; Fan Yi asserts power on the system which oppresses her but simultaneously victimizes herself by perusing Chou Ping.


The Liang Zhu Drama Society, formed following the success of last year's production Butterfly Lovers, strives to introduce Chinese drama to Western society, encouraging an assimilation of culture between the East and the West. Student writers translate the scripts to English.

This production of Thunderstorm, billed as "The Chinese answer to Shakespeare", accomplish a mingling of Eastern and Western drama of many levels, the dazzling array of authentic Chinese costumes combined with a multi-cultured cast and a morphing of Chinese dramatic gestures with English drama contribute to the culmination of assimilatory efforts well worth ditching the books and the beer for a night.
